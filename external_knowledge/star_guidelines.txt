STAR GUIDELINES FOR INTERVIEW ANSWERS

STAR stands for:
- Situation
- Task
- Action
- Result

It is a structured way to answer behavioral and project-based interview questions.

--------------------------------------------------
1. SITUATION
--------------------------------------------------
Explain the context briefly.
Answer:
- Where were you?
- What project or task were you working on?
- What was happening?

Example:
During my Master’s thesis at Ca’ Foscari University, I worked on a 3D group detection project using LiDAR-based pedestrian detections.

Keep it short. Do not spend too much time here.

--------------------------------------------------
2. TASK
--------------------------------------------------
Explain your responsibility or objective.
Answer:
- What were you expected to do?
- What problem were you trying to solve?
- What was your specific role?

Example:
My task was to design a lightweight method to identify pedestrian groups using only geometric proximity, without relying on semantic cues or training-based methods.

This part clarifies what YOU had to do.

--------------------------------------------------
3. ACTION
--------------------------------------------------
Explain what you actually did.
Answer:
- What method did you choose?
- What steps did you follow?
- What tools or technologies did you use?
- Why did you choose that approach?

Example:
I designed a pipeline that processed LiDAR-based detections and grouped them using DBSCAN clustering. I tested how a distance-based method could work under practical constraints and focused on making the system simple and interpretable.

This is usually the most important part.

--------------------------------------------------
4. RESULT
--------------------------------------------------
Explain the outcome.
Answer:
- What happened in the end?
- What did you learn?
- What improvement or value came from your work?

Example:
The result was a lightweight 3D group detection approach suitable for real-time scenarios such as robotics or surveillance. I also learned how to make technical design decisions based on both performance and simplicity.

This part should show impact and reflection.

--------------------------------------------------
HOW TO USE STAR WELL
--------------------------------------------------
1. Focus on your contribution, not only the team.
2. Keep the answer structured and easy to follow.
3. Use concrete technical details where useful.
4. End with a result or lesson learned.
5. Avoid being too vague.

--------------------------------------------------
STAR EXAMPLE 1
Question: Tell me about a project you are proud of.
--------------------------------------------------
Situation:
During my Master’s thesis, I worked on a 3D group detection problem using LiDAR-based detections.

Task:
I needed to create a lightweight system for identifying pedestrian groups without depending on semantic cues or model retraining.

Action:
I designed a distance-based clustering pipeline using DBSCAN and focused on a simple geometric approach. I also thought carefully about how the method could be useful in crowded scenes and practical deployment scenarios.

Result:
The final system was lightweight and suitable for real-time contexts. It also helped me improve my skills in computer vision pipeline design and technical evaluation.

--------------------------------------------------
STAR EXAMPLE 2
Question: Describe a challenge you faced.
--------------------------------------------------
Situation:
While working on AI projects, I often needed to move from theory to implementation quickly.

Task:
One challenge was choosing an approach that was both technically valid and practically manageable.

Action:
Instead of selecting a highly complex method immediately, I first built a minimal working version, tested its behavior, and improved it step by step.

Result:
This helped me make better design decisions and complete projects more effectively, while also learning how to balance simplicity and performance.
